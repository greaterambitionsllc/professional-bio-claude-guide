Your bio is not a resume summary. It is a positioning document. Most professionals get this wrong, and it costs them opportunities they never see.

Decision makers read your bio before they meet you. Conference organizers read it before they book you. Clients read it before they call you. If your bio recites job titles instead of stating the problem you solve, it does the opposite of its job. This guide shows you how to use Claude.AI to build a bio that positions you correctly, in your own voice, in about an hour.

Section 01

Why Most Professional Bios Fail

The standard professional bio is a chronological recap. Name, title, years of experience, list of employers, degree, closing line about family or hobbies. It is accurate. It is also forgettable.

A bio fails when it answers the wrong question. The reader is not asking "what has this person done?" The reader is asking "what can this person do for me?" Those are different questions, and they require different documents.

AI changes the economics of fixing this. What used to take a branding consultant and three revision cycles now takes a structured conversation with Claude. But the tool only works if you feed it the right inputs and ask in the right sequence. That sequence is what this guide gives you.

Section 02

Gather Your Raw Material First

Claude cannot invent your career. The quality of your bio is capped by the quality of what you give it. Before you open a chat, collect these six inputs into one document. Bullet points are fine. Polish is not required.

Input 01

Outcomes, Not Duties

Three to five results with numbers attached. Budget recovered, revenue grown, time saved, teams scaled. Specificity does the persuading.

Input 02

Your Audience

Who reads this bio and what decision are they making? A client bio, a speaker bio, and a board bio are not the same document.

Input 03

Credentials That Matter

Degrees, certifications, clearances. List them all now. You will cut later. The bio will use the two or three your audience respects most.

Input 04

Your Point of View

One belief about your field that most peers would not say out loud. This is what makes a bio memorable instead of interchangeable.

Input 05

Proof of Authority

Publications, speaking engagements, leadership roles, media mentions, awards. External validation the reader can verify.

Input 06

The Human Line

One sentence of texture: where you are based, what you build outside work, what you are writing. One sentence. Not a paragraph.

Section 03

The Five-Prompt Bio System

Run these prompts in Claude.AI in order, in a single conversation. Each prompt builds on the last. Copy them exactly, then replace the bracketed text with your own material.

1

Set the Role and Load Your Material

This prompt turns Claude into a positioning strategist instead of a generic writer. It also forces an interview phase, which surfaces details you forgot you had.

Prompt 1 · SetupYou are an executive positioning strategist who writes professional bios for senior leaders. Your job is to position me for [TARGET AUDIENCE, e.g., CEOs of mid-size tech companies evaluating consultants]. Here is my raw material: [PASTE YOUR SIX INPUTS FROM SECTION 02] Before writing anything, ask me 5 questions that would help you position me more sharply. Focus on gaps in my material, not what I already gave you.
2

Answer the Questions, Then Request the First Draft

Answer Claude's questions honestly. Short answers are fine. Then request the draft with explicit structural rules so you get a positioning document, not a chronology.

Prompt 2 · First DraftNow write my full professional bio, 250 to 300 words, third person. Rules: 1. Open with the problem I solve or the value I create. Never open with my name and title. 2. Use my actual numbers. Do not soften or generalize them. 3. Credentials appear through specifics, not adjectives. Never call me "seasoned," "passionate," or "results-driven." 4. Include my point of view in one sentence. 5. End with one line of human texture. 6. Short declarative sentences. No em dashes.
3

Pressure-Test the Draft

Do not accept the first draft. Make Claude attack its own work. This is where the generic phrasing gets stripped out.

Prompt 3 · CritiqueCritique this bio as a skeptical [TARGET READER, e.g., Fortune 500 COO] would. Identify: 1. Every phrase that could appear in anyone's bio 2. Every claim that lacks proof 3. The single weakest sentence 4. What is missing that this reader would want to know Then rewrite the bio fixing all four issues.
4

Match It to Your Voice

A bio that does not sound like you fails in person. Give Claude a writing sample and let it close the gap between the draft and how you actually communicate.

Prompt 4 · VoiceHere is a sample of my actual writing: [PASTE A LINKEDIN POST, EMAIL, OR ARTICLE YOU WROTE] Compare the bio's voice against my writing. Adjust sentence length, word choice, and tone so the bio sounds like me on my best day. Keep all facts and structure intact.
5

Generate Every Length You Need

One conversation, every deliverable. Claude already holds your full context, so the short versions stay consistent with the long one.

Prompt 5 · VersionsUsing the final bio, produce: 1. SHORT BIO: 100 words, for speaker introductions and proposals 2. ONE-LINER: 25 words or fewer, for panels and podcast intros 3. LINKEDIN ABOUT: 150 words, first person, written to my target audience 4. SOCIAL BIO: 160 characters, for X/Instagram profiles Each version must lead with value, not title.

Section 04

Six Mistakes That Sink Professional Bios

Review your final output against this list. If Claude produced any of these, send it back with the specific fix.

Opening with your name and title

The reader can see your name. Open with the problem you solve. Your name belongs in sentence two or three.

Adjective-based credibility

"Dynamic," "seasoned," and "passionate" are claims without evidence. A $38M program recovery is evidence. State the number and delete the adjective.

Listing every employer

Your bio is not a resume. Name two or three organizations your audience recognizes. The rest is noise.

Writing one bio for every context

A speaker bio sells the talk. A client bio sells the engagement. A board bio sells judgment. Different readers, different documents.

Accepting Claude's first draft

The first draft is raw material. The system works because of Prompts 3 and 4. Skipping the critique step produces a polished version of generic.

Letting it go stale

A bio with no results from the last 18 months reads as a career in past tense. Re-run Prompt 5 twice a year with new wins loaded.

Section 05

The Final Quality Check

Before your bio goes anywhere public, it must pass every line of this checklist. No exceptions.

The first sentence states value, not identity. A stranger reading only that sentence knows what you do for people like them.

At least two claims carry numbers. Dollars, percentages, team sizes, timelines. Verifiable specifics.

Zero filler adjectives. Search for "passionate," "dynamic," "seasoned," and "results-driven." Delete every instance.

It sounds like you out loud. Read it to someone who knows you. If they smirk, revise.

Every fact is accurate. Claude drafts from what you provide. You are the verification step. Check every number, title, and organization name before publishing.

All four lengths exist and agree. Full, short, one-liner, and social versions tell the same story at different depths.

The bio is the smallest document in your professional portfolio and the one read most often. Treat it like infrastructure, not an afterthought.
